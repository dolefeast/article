\section{Discussion and outlook}
\label{sec:discussion}
Ambjørn \& Wolfram~\cite{ambjorn_properties_1983} studied the effect of backreaction of a charged quantum scalar field due to  opposite charges placed on the boundaries of a finite 1-dimensional region. 
Instabilities appearing in the external field approximation are studied, along with their resolution through vacuum polarization via an     iterative procedure.
It was pointed out later that their prescription $\rho ^\text{MS}$, based on a mode sum formula, leads to wrong results \cite{schlemmer_current_2015}.
The correct prescription $\rho$ introduced in
\cite{wernersson_vacuum_2021} finds discrepancies of approximately an order of magnitude with $\rho^\text{MS}$, in the external field approximation. 
In this paper, we  revisited the semiclassical KGM equations studied by Ambjørn \textit{et al.}, with the correct prescription for $\rho$ under  varying boundary conditions and masses of the scalar field. 
%For this, we have introduced the physical set up, a finite 1-dimensional region with a constant background field generated by two classical charges at the boundaries, and employed a mode ansatz to decompose the field solution $\phi$. 


Our primary finding is that the backreaction through $\rho$ also avoids the instabilities that appear in the external field approximation. However, we observe that beyond the critical field, the total electric field does not stabilize at a fixed value; instead, it recedes. This may indicate a breakdown of the semiclassical approximation, as the quantum fluctuations of the electric field are neglected.

Interestingly, when we compare the results using the correct prescription with those obtained with the older method, their strong-field behaviors are found to be indistinguishable. This is attributed to the low-energy modes being the most sensitive to the background field, which are precisely the modes that dominate the onset of runaway solutions. 
  
Future work could focus on benchmarking the semiclassical approximation with scalar QED. This  is a key comparison for delineating the regime of validity of the semiclassical approach.
