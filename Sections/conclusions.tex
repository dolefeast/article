\section{Discussion and outlook}

~\cite{ambjorn_properties_1983} studied the effect of backreaction of a charged quantum scalar field on a background classical constant electric field in a finite region. It was found that vacuum polarization raises the energy of the quantum field modes, thus  avoiding runaway solutions that otherwise appear.
It was later pointed out \cite{schlemmer_current_2015} that their prescription for vacuum polarization, relying on a mode sum expansion leads to wrong results. 
The correct prescription is introduced in
\cite{wernersson_vacuum_2021} which defines vacuum polarization $\rho$  in a manifestly gauge-invariant manner through point-splitting w.r.t.~a Hadamard parametrix and finds discrepancies of approximately an order of magnitude between the two prescriptions. 

In this paper, we have revisited the semiclassical KGM equations studied by Ambjorn \textit{et al.}, with the correct prescription for $\rho$.  We have systematically explored varying boundary conditions and masses of the scalar field. For this, we have introduced the physical set up, a finite 1-dimensional region with a constant background field generated by two classical charges at the boundaries, and employed a mode ansatz to decompose the field solution $\phi$. The correct prescription of $\rho$ in \cite{wernersson_vacuum_2021} is reviewed. This relies on the assumption that the vacuum state of the scalar field is Hadamard. This allows us to use the Hadamard parametrix, a state-independent, locally constructed object, as the counterterm in the point-splitting renormalization procedure used in defining $\rho$. Finally, the iterative procedure to solve the KGM equations for a given background field is outlined, and the solution space is traversed by a numerical continuation method. 


Our primary finding is that the backreaction through $\rho$ also avoids the instabilities that otherwise appear. However, we observe that beyond the critical field, the total electric field does not stabilize at a fixed value; instead, it recedes. This might indicate a breakdown of the semiclassical approximation, as the quantum fluctuations of the eletric field are neglected.

Interestingly, when we compare the results using the correct prescription with those obtained with the older method, their strong-field behaviors are found to be indistinguishable. This is attributed to the low energy modes being the most sensitive to the background field, which are precisely the modes that dominate the onset of runaway solutions. 
 
Future work could focus on benchmarking the semiclassical approximation with scalar QED. This  is a key comparison delineating the regime of validity of the semiclassical approach.
