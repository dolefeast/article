\section{Set up} 

In 1+1 dimensional spacetime, two charges $q, -q$
are set at $x^1=0, x^{1}=a$, respectively. Ignoring vacuum polarization effects,
this yields a constant electric field of strength $q$ pointing towards positive $x^{1}$ in the region $x^1\in[0, a]$, 0 everywhere else.
We study the solutions to the semiclassical Klein-Gordon-Maxwell equations (KGM), i.e.~the system of coupled differential equations
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        \left( D_\mu D^\mu + m^2 \right)\phi &= 0, 
        \label{eq:KG}\\
        \partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} &= \left< j^\nu \right>,
        \label{eq:Maxwell}
    \end{align}
\end{subequations} in the region $x=(x^0, x^1) \in     \mathbb{R} \times [0,
a]$. Here, $D_\mu = \partial_\mu +ie A_\mu$ is the gauge covariant
derivative, \begin{align}
    j^\nu(x) = \frac{ie}{2} \left((D^\nu \phi^*(x)) \phi (x)- \phi^*(x)
    D^\nu \phi(x) \right)
    \label{eq:charge-current-density}
\end{align}
is the charge current density of the Klein-Gordon field, and
$\left< \mathcal{O}\right>$ is the vacuum expectation value of an observable $\mathcal{O},$ and the metric signature
is $(+,-)$.
We solve for $F_{\mu\nu}$ and (the mode decomposition of) the two-point function of the vacuum state.

In 1+1 dimensions, $A_1$ can be arbitrarily set to 0. This way, Eq.~\eqref{eq:Maxwell} reduces to the Poisson equation for the electrostatic potential $A_0$
\begin{align}
    \partial_1^2 A_0(x^1) = -\left< j^0 \right> \equiv -\rho, \label{eq:poisson}
\end{align} 
with $\rho$ the vacuum polarization, i.e.~the vacuum expectation value of the zeroth component of the charge current density.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:poisson} will be solved with the boundary conditions
\begin{align}
    \left. \partial_1
        A_0(x^1)\right|_{x^1=0, a} =  -q.
        \label{eq:A0-boundary-conditions}
\end{align}
This way, measuring the electric field close enough to the boundary is not affected by vacuum polarization.
The boundary conditions \eqref{eq:A0-boundary-conditions} fix the solution to Eq.~\eqref{eq:poisson} up to an addition constant,
which is for convenience chosen so that $A_0(\frac{a}{2}) = 0$. With these boundary conditions and for a given $\rho(x^1)$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:poisson} is solved by
\begin{align}
        A_0(x^1) = -q\left(x^1 - \frac{a}{2} \right) -
        \int_{\frac{a}{2}}^{x^{1}} \int_{0}^{\chi} \rho(\xi) d \xi d \chi.
    \label{eq:A0-general} 
\end{align}


\subsection{Field modes}

In this gauge, the Klein-Gordon equation is of the form \begin{align}
    \left[ (\partial_0 + ie A_0 )^2 + \partial_1^2 - m^2
    \right]\phi = 0,
    \label{eq:TDKGE}
\end{align}
which we will study using Dirichlet boundary conditions (DBC) $\left.\phi \right|_{x^{1}=0,a} = 0$ or Neumman boundary conditions (NBC) $\left.\partial_{1} \phi\right|_{x^1=0,a }=0$. More general boundary conditions may be considered, though they are outside of the scope of this work.
Using the mode ansatz $\phi_n(x) = \phi_n (x^{1}) e^{-i\Omega_n x^0}$  the PDE is split into ordinary differential equations for each mode $\phi_n (x^{1})$ of the scalar field
\begin{align}
    \left[ (\Omega_n - e A_0 )^2 + \partial_{1}^2 -  m^2 \right]\phi_n
    = 0.
    \label{eq:TIKGE}
\end{align}
The following dimensionless parameters are defined
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
z &= a^{-1}x^1\\
\lambda &= e E a^2\\
\varepsilon &= ae\\
\omega_{n} &= a\Omega_{n, \alpha}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:TIKGE} then takes the form \begin{align}
    \left[ (\omega_n - \varepsilon A_0 )^2 + \frac{d^2}{dz^2} - a^2
    m^2\right] \phi_n = 0.  \label{eq:mode-equation}
\end{align}
The modes $\phi_n$ are normalized with respect to the symplectic
norm
\begin{align}
    (\phi_n, \phi_m) = i\int_{0}^{1} (\phi_n^* \pi_m^* - \pi_n \phi_m)dz,
\end{align}
with $\pi_n = (D_0 \phi_n)^*$ the canonical conjugate of
$\phi_n$.

The general solution to Eq.~\eqref{eq:TDKGE} is thus decomposed in
modes \begin{align}
    \phi(t, z) = \sum_{n>0}^{} a_n \phi_n(z) e^{-i\omega_n t} +
    \sum_{n<0}^{} b^\dagger_n \phi_n(z) e^{-i\omega_n t}.
    \label{eq:mode-decomposition}
\end{align} Positive (negative) values of $n$ are identified with modes of positive
(negative) norm. $a_n$ and $b_n$ are the ladder operators for the positive and
negative norm solutions, respectively, obeying canonical commutation relations
$
    [a_n, a_n^\dagger ] = [b_n, b_n^\dagger ] =\delta_{nm},
$
and all other commutators vanishing. The ladder operators define
the vacuum state $\ket{0}$ as that for which $a \ket{0} = b\ket{0} = 0$. Note that due to the chosen gauge, $\omega_n = -\omega_{-n},$ and the $b_n^\dagger$  are the creation operators of the negative frequency family of solutions. 

As mentioned above, the definition of quantities such as $\rho$ \textemdash Wick powers\textemdash is problematic, since it involves the product of local observables 
evaluated at coinciding spacetime points (see Eq.~\eqref{eq:charge-current-density}).
To define such polynomials, one assumes that the states of a field $\phi$ are Hadamard, i.e.~they have a similar short-distance behavior to the vacuum of  $\phi$ in absence of background fields, and are assumed to be the only physically relevant states~\cite{fewster_necessity_2013}. Under this assumption, the divergence of such states is state independenent and locally constructed from the background fields. Since $\phi$ is coupled to the background field through the covariant derivative, one may use the setup at hand as a toy model for semiclassical gravity, a possible first step towards an elusive quantum theory of gravity.

Specifically, one says that the two point function  $w_{\Omega}^{\phi \phi^*}(x, x') = \braket{\Omega | \phi (x)\phi^* (x')| \Omega} $ of the scalar field $\phi$ in
a Hadamard state $\Omega$  is of the form
\begin{align}
    w_{\Omega}^{\phi\phi^*}(x, x') 
    \equiv H^{\phi \phi^*}(x, x')  + R^{\phi \phi^*}_{\Omega} (x, x').
    \label{eq:hadamard-condition}
\end{align}
Here,
\begin{equation}
    H^{\phi\phi^*} = -\frac{V(x,x')}{4\pi} \ln [(t-t')^2 -
    (z-z')^2 + i\varepsilon \text{sign} (t-t')]
    \label{eq:hadamard-parametrix}
\end{equation}
is the Hadamard parametrix of the scalar field in 1+1 spacetime dimensions and $R_{\Omega}^{\phi\phi^*}$
a state-dependent smooth function. 
The smooth coefficient $V$, up to order relevant for vacuum polarization reads \cite{schlemmer_current_2015}
\begin{align*}
    V(x, x') &= \exp\left[-i\varepsilon \int_{0}^{1} A_{\mu}(x' +
    s(x-x') ) (x - x')^{\mu}ds \right] \\&+ \mathcal{O}(x-x')^2.
\end{align*} 
The Hadamard condition allows us to define expectation values of
local observables non-linear in the fields as a limit of coinciding points:
\begin{align}
\begin{split}
         \braket{D_\alpha \phi (x) D^{*}_\beta \phi^*(x)} & \\= \lim_{x' \to x}
    D_\alpha D^{*}_\beta{}' &(w_\Omega^{\phi \phi^*}(x, x')- H^{\phi\phi^*}(x,
    x')),
\end{split}
    \label{eq:point-splitting}
\end{align}
with $\alpha, \beta$ symmetrized multiindices.

Eqs.~\eqref{eq:point-splitting}, \eqref{eq:charge-current-density} together with  the mode decomposition \eqref{eq:mode-decomposition}  result in the expression for $\rho$ calculated in \cite{wernersson_vacuum_2021}
\begin{align}
\begin{split}
        \rho =& \lim_{\tau \to 0} \sum_{n>0}^{} \Big[(\omega_n - \varepsilon A_0) \lvert
    \phi_n\rvert^2 e^{-i\omega_n \tau}\\ 
    &+\sum_{n<0}^{} (\omega_n - \varepsilon
    A_0) \lvert \phi_n\rvert^2 e^{i\omega_n \tau} \Big]+ \frac{\varepsilon^2}{\pi}
    A_0 .
    \end{split} 
   \label{eq:vacuum-polarization-long}
\end{align}
An example of $\rho$ using this expression is given in
Fig.~\ref{fig:Figures-dirichlet-unfilteredRhoaa-pdf}, calculated in the external field approximation explained below, i.e.~a homogeneous background electric field given by the potential $A_0(z) = -0.5(z-0.5)$.

To measure the \textit{strength} of the vacuum polarization, the \textit{induced charge}
\begin{align}
    Q_I = \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{2}} \rho(z) dz
    \label{eq:polarized charge}
\end{align}
is defined. This charge partially screens the background electric field $\lambda$,
and Gauß's law yields that the total electric field at $z=\frac{1}{2}$ as
$\lambda + Q_I$.

\label{subsec:External-Field-Approximation}

of the charged scalar field to the background electric field. This
approximation displays the instabilities of the system, which the inclusion of
backreaction aims to avoid. Eq.~\eqref{eq:mode-equation} is solved with
$A_0(z) = -\lambda (z - \frac{1}{2})$, and takes the form

\subsection{Mode sum formula}
\label{sec:mode-sum-formula}
Vacuum polarization in \cite{ambjorn_properties_1983} is defined through a so-called mode sum formula. It pairs the charge density associated to each mode as
\begin{align}
    \rho_n = (\omega_n-\varepsilon A_0)\lvert \phi_n \rvert^2  -(\omega_n+\varepsilon A_0)\lvert \phi_{-n} \rvert^2.
    \label{eq:mode-charge-density}
\end{align}
to define the vacuum polarization 
\begin{align}
\begin{split}
        \rho^\text{MS} &= \sum_{n>0}^{} \rho_n \\&= \sum_{n>0}^{}\big[(\omega_n-\varepsilon A_0)\lvert \phi_n \rvert^2
    - ( \omega_n +\varepsilon A_0) \lvert\phi_{-n}\rvert^2
    \big],
\end{split}
    \label{eq:mode-sum}    
\end{align}
where the superscript MS is introduced to differentiate it from the correct prescription $\rho$.
% The above expression cannot be obtained from a manifestly gauge invariant renormalization scheme, and also relies on a specific pairing of the modes in the series to be convergent. 
The above expression cannot be derived from a manifestly gauge invariant renormalization scheme, and it relies on the specific pairing of modes to be well-defined. 
Furthermore, in the iterative procedure discussed above,~\cite{ambjorn_properties_1983} truncates the mode sum to $\rho^\text{MS} \approx \rho_1$. 

Fig.~\ref{fig:hambjorn} shows different charge densities arising from the different $\rho$ prescriptions, for the massless scalar field subject to DBC. The same $\phi_n$, $\omega_n$ and \linebreak$A_0(z) = -(z-\frac{1}{2})$ have been used for the different $\rho$. In blue, the vacuum polarization used in the computations throughout this paper, in orange the vacuum polarization arising from the mode pairing and in red the one used for the iterative procedure in \cite{ambjorn_properties_1983}. 
Notice the non-zero vacuum polarization at the boundaries for Eq.~\eqref{eq:mode-sum}, which the reader might find surprising to find in a field subject to DBC. This is an artifact of the missing $\frac{\varepsilon^2}{\pi}A_0$ term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mode-sum}. 

We observe that $\rho$ is significantly `weaker' than the one used by \cite{ambjorn_properties_1983}, where it was seen that the screening due to $\rho^{\text{MS}}$ avoids the instabilities appearing in the external field approximation. This motivates the question answered in this paper: Does considering the correct vacuum polarization $\rho$ also avoid these instabilities?
